Sam Altman, Sundar Pichai, and Demis Hassabis Were All at India's AI Summit. The Most Important Launch May Have Been One They Missed.

FEB 23, 2026 • ORIGINALLY PUBLISHED BY ANI • 6 MIN READ

When India opened Bharat Mandapam for the India AI Impact Summit 2026, the message was scale: more than 3,250 speakers across hundreds of sessions from February 16 to 20, delegations from 100+ countries, and twenty world leaders in attendance.

The Summit That Redefined Scale

Union IT Minister Ashwini Vaishnaw said AI infrastructure commitments tied to the summit crossed $250 billion in aggregate, driven by announcements across data centres, cloud, and compute. Among them: Reliance and Jio's plan to invest Rs 10 lakh crore over seven years, Adani's $100 billion commitment for AI-ready data centres by 2035, and major India investments disclosed by Microsoft, Google, and Amazon. OpenAI partnered with Tata Group. Anthropic partnered with Infosys.

"Advanced AI could have ten times the impact of the Industrial Revolution, at ten times the speed."
— Demis Hassabis, CEO of Google DeepMind

The Launch They Missed

But the article's headline argues the most important launch at the summit may have been overlooked by the big names. On Day 3, Abhilash Chadhar, a former Intel engineer, was focused on a question the summit largely skipped: why is chip design still shaped by workflows and tool architectures built decades ago?

Chadhar's answer is ChipOS, built by his Netherlands-headquartered company FutureAtoms. The company describes it as an agentic operating system for semiconductor design: not a chat interface, but a platform that orchestrates tasks across the design cycle from a single interface.

FutureAtoms says engineers can describe intent in plain English, while AI agents generate RTL and verification scaffolding, run tests, triage failures, and iterate through debug loops.

Why Chip Design Needs Disruption

  • Synopsys notes that 60% of functional verification time is spent on testbench development and debug alone
  • Siemens has said logic verification can consume over 70% of the overall IC development cycle
  • The market is anchored by U.S.-based Synopsys and Cadence, and Siemens EDA (European-owned), with workflows involving manual handoffs between tools, scripts, teams, and documents

C-DAC Meets FutureAtoms

C-DAC, the agency behind India's DHRUV64 RISC-V processor programme (launched December 2025), met with FutureAtoms at the summit to discuss a potential evaluation of ChipOS for India's indigenous processor roadmap, FutureAtoms said.

Geopolitics and Semiconductor Sovereignty

During summit week, India joined the U.S.-led Pax Silica initiative. In remarks reported by ANI, U.S. AI advisor Sriram Krishnan said: "We want to make sure that the world uses the American AI stack."

Against that geopolitical backdrop, the question of who controls the tools that design the chips powering AI becomes even more significant. FutureAtoms is positioning ChipOS as an alternative approach: an agentic platform that gives design teams autonomy over their workflows, rather than locking them into legacy EDA vendor ecosystems.

What ChipOS Offers

FutureAtoms lists a free tier (one knowledge source, 50 queries per day), a $29 per month Pro plan, and enterprise options including on-premise and air-gapped deployments. The platform launched publicly at the India AI Impact Summit 2026.

ChipOS Capabilities

  • Describe design intent in plain English
  • AI agents generate RTL and verification scaffolding
  • Automated test execution and failure triage
  • Iterative debug loops with AI assistance
  • Single-platform orchestration of the entire design cycle
  • On-premise and air-gapped deployment options for enterprise
